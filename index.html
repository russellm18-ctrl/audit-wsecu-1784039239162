## Atmospheric Reality Gap

Your hero image — wind turbines rolling across open Washington hills at golden hour — is genuinely well-composed photography. Clean light, real depth, a landscape that reads Pacific Northwest without a caption. That image earns attention. The problem is what follows it.

The second hero frame drops to a woman and a dog in a field with a flat gray sky hammered over it and a yellow dot-matrix graphic overlay bleeding into the frame. The overlay doesn't clarify a message — it obscures a photograph that was already doing emotional work. You are paying a designer to sabotage your photographer. The signal that reaches a prospective member: this organization is not sure what it wants to say.

The third frame — the credit card product shot — is clean and brand-consistent. But the fourth frame, showing a handshake across a table, is underlit, soft-focused, and shot at a compression that makes every face read as a stock photo placeholder. A member weighing a mortgage decision or a business account does not feel seen in that room. They feel like a generic outcome.

The trust cost: you have four frames that disagree with each other on quality, intent, and finish. When a buyer can't find a through-line in your visuals, they resolve the uncertainty by choosing a competitor whose presentation is coherent.

## Visual Narrative Coherence

Your link audit returned zero broken links across 25 checked — that infrastructure is sound. But the fracture isn't in dead pages; it's in the visual handoff between frames. The wind turbine image promises a Washington-rooted institution with a long view. The overlaid dot-wave graphic pattern reappears on both the hero and the lifestyle frame, which creates a brand system — but it lands differently each time because the underlying photographs don't share a consistent color grade, focal distance, or light quality. The graphic layer is being used to paper over photographic inconsistency rather than amplify a coherent one.

A high-value buyer — someone moving their business account, consolidating debt, or deciding between you and a regional bank — spends about eight seconds on a homepage before forming a trust judgment. When the visual quality shifts frame-to-frame, they register instability, not variety.

## Metadata and Conversion Gaps

This is where real money is leaking. Of your 21 content images, 20 are missing alt text — a 95% gap. That means the hero wind turbine image, the lifestyle shot, the credit card product frame, and the member consultation photo are all invisible to search engines. You rank #1 for "WSECU" because people already know your name. But for every search that isn't branded — "Washington state credit union," "best credit union Washington," "low rate credit card Washington" — your images contribute nothing to your relevance signal.

Your meta description is missing entirely. Google is pulling arbitrary page text to fill that slot in search results, which means you have surrendered control of your first impression to an algorithm. Your page title at 46 characters is serviceable, but without a meta description, click-through from non-branded searches drops measurably. The og:image tag is present, which protects your social share appearance — but that's a floor, not a ceiling.

File naming on your images was not surfaced in the scan, so that vector cannot be confirmed — but given 95% missing alt text, the probability of keyword-rich filenames is low.

You are currently paying for photography that search engines cannot read and a brand system that graphic overlays are holding together by force. Neither problem requires new photography to fix — but both require a decision before the next campaign spend adds to the gap.
